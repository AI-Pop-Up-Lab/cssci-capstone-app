Every freight schedule starts with the big picture: the railway and road operators publish a master timetable that shows when trains and trucks are allowed on each line, what slots are open for loading and unloading, and when maintenance windows will close the routes. From there I build the daily schedule for the loads we have to move. I match each shipment’s pickup and delivery points with the available slots, making sure the timing fits the customers’ windows and the capacity of the vehicles we have. If a load needs a specific type of wagon or a refrigerated truck, I note that too, so the dispatcher can assign the right equipment.

Coordination is a two‑way street. I send my draft to the transport operators – the rail company, the trucking firms, and sometimes the port authority – and they confirm whether the slots are still free. They may suggest a slight shift if a higher‑priority cargo has been booked or if a crew change is scheduled. I adjust the plan, lock it in, and then distribute the final schedule to the drivers, the warehouse staff, and the customers.

Even a small delay can ripple through the whole chain – a missed slot at the distribution centre means the next load has to wait, which can push back deliveries to the shops, including my wife’s bakery.

When everything runs on time, the schedule keeps costs predictable, the supply chain stays smooth, and the customers get their goods when they expect them. When a delay slips in, the impact spreads quickly, which is why I check the schedule every morning, stay in touch with the operators throughout the day, and always have a backup plan ready.
